Understanding Linear Algebra

Linear algebra is a tool that is widely used in engineering and the sciences. It can be used in computer graphics to transform a scene to an orientation that can be projected onto the screen. Linear algebra can be used to model the vibration of structures or predict the current and voltage in an analog electric circuit. It can be used to predict the orbital shapes of an electron. The uses of linear algebra a diverse, and a good understanding of linear algrbra can be useful for understanding its applications.

One of the challenges to understanding linear algebra is that there are a lot of computations, typically performed using matrices and vectors. The typical course presents the method for performing computations, but spends little time helping the student develop intuition as to what the computations mean. As opposed to calculus where you shown graphically what a derivative is, or vector algebra where you are shown how vectors can be added or subtracted and are provided graphical representation of dot products and cross products, in linear algebra it is common to include few graphical representations. When graphical representation are provided, a student is not always provided with the insight to be able to relate the picture to the matrix used to derive the picture. As a result, you might feel as lost in a sea of computation as I did when I first learned linear algebra. I could perform the computation, but I had little idea of what kind of results to expect.

The lack of graphical representation in linear algebra is unfortunate because many of the fundamental concepts of linear algebra have simple and intuitive graphical representations that can provide a student with useful insights. The objective of this material is to provide the student of linear algebra with some understanding of the graphical foundations of linear algebra so that they can more effectively understand some of the core principles of linear algebra.
